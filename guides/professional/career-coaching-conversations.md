# Career Coaching Conversations

Guide for preparing quarterly (or ad hoc) career coaching conversations between Gabe and a direct report.

Distinct from:
- **Performance reviews** (formal, cyclical, written). See `manager-performance-reviews.md`.
- **1:1s** (weekly, tactical).

A career coaching conversation is forward-looking. It's about direction, growth, and the next rep. It's not a status meeting.

### Special case: the first career coaching after a review

If the last career touchpoint was the written review itself (no separate post-review conversation happened), this meeting is two conversations in one: review debrief + forward-looking. Do not treat the review feedback as already-discussed. It's been read, not spoken.

When this is the case:
- Order matters. Review debrief first, direction second, experiment third.
- Weight the review-debrief sections heavily. Each development area gets its own named sub-section with an opening line Gabe should use.
- Book 75 minutes, not 60. Be willing to split into two meetings if the debrief runs long.
- Name it openly: "This is the first time we're talking through the review live. I want to make sure it lands, and then talk about where you want to go."

**How to detect this case while prepping:** check for a career conversation prep doc dated after the most recent review's issue date. If none exists, or the most recent one was drafted but the meeting didn't happen (ask Gabe if uncertain), this is the first-post-review case.

### Special case: the first review with a new direct report (no prior review exists)

If no prior review exists in this reporting relationship, the conversation is three things at once:
1. **Discovery.** Gabe doesn't know this person's career direction yet.
2. **Calibration.** This IS the first structured review-type conversation. They need a clear read from Gabe, grounded in specific examples.
3. **Forward direction.** Where are they headed, what's the Q2 bet.

When this is the case:
- The brag sheet is the substrate, not a prior review. Read it in full before writing.
- Do not invent development feedback. If Gabe hasn't observed something himself, the prep should not fabricate it from brag-sheet patterns. Better to note "development area TBD, next quarter" than manufacture criticism.
- Open with the person's own self-read first ("What are you proudest of, where did you come up short?") before Gabe's calibration. This builds the discovery into the meeting.
- Calibration section in the prep lists 2-3 named strengths with specific examples pulled from the brag sheet, and at most 1-2 development areas only if Gabe has observed them.
- If the brag sheet shows the person operating at lead or manager scope without a matching title, name that explicitly as a "title / level / scope alignment" follow-up item.
- Flag brag sheet staleness. Offer the refresh command: `/perf-feedback:brag-sheet` with the appropriate window.

**How to detect this case while prepping:** check for any file under `people/team/<name>/reviews/`. If none exists, this is the first-review case.

## Principles

1. **Direction before development.** Don't open with feedback. Open with where they want to go. Development areas only make sense against a direction.
2. **One experiment, not a list.** Leave with one specific, time-boxed growth bet. Not three. Not five. One. If they want to do more, that's their call, but the prep names one.
3. **Observable outcomes.** Every commitment ends with a line you could check in 60 days: "what tells us this worked?"
4. **Energy is a signal.** Notice whether they're building or leaking. If they're depleted, the career question changes shape.
5. **Name the gap honestly.** If they're asking about a promotion they're not tracking toward, say so. Don't dodge with process talk.
6. **Silence is fine.** Don't fill it.

---

## Inputs to Gather Before Prep

1. Name, role, level.
2. Most recent performance review (manager feedback + self-review + peer feedback if available).
3. Any prior career conversation prep docs for this person.
4. Your notes from recent 1:1s or observed behavior since the last career chat.
5. Anything they've said publicly (Slack, design docs, reviews) that signals direction or energy.

If there's no prior review, ask Gabe for: (a) what they're great at, (b) what would unlock them, (c) what he's heard from skip-levels or peers.

## Structure of the Prep Doc

Save to: `people/team/<name>/career-conversations/<YYYY-MM-DD>-prep.md`.

Sections, in this order:

### 1. Header
- Date of the meeting.
- Cadence (quarterly, post-review, ad hoc).
- One-line context: what's different since last time.

### 2. Your Goal for This Conversation
Three outcomes you want. Not a list of topics. Outcomes. Example:
- A clear, stated 18-24 month career direction.
- One growth experiment for the quarter.
- A read on their energy.

### 3. What Changed Since Last Time
Bulleted. Observed reality, not promises. Placeholders with `_fill in_` are fine if Gabe will complete them right before the meeting.

If there was no prior conversation, skip this section.

### 4. Open With: Energy and Direction
Actual opening question, written verbatim. Things to listen for. Remind the prep-reader not to fill silence.

### 5. The Core Question: Direction
One big question with verbatim wording. Follow-ups conditional on their answer:
- If they name a bigger-scope direction: probe specifics.
- If they name current-scope depth: probe what gets better.
- If they hedge: offer two concrete options to react to.

### 6. Growth Experiment for the Quarter
Menu of 3-5 options to offer, ranked by what their development feedback suggests they need. Each option:
- The experiment in one line.
- What it addresses (tie to review feedback).

Do not assign. Offer. Let them pick.

Then: what "specific" means. The four questions every commitment must answer:
- When does it start?
- First concrete action in the next 7 days?
- What observable outcome at the end?
- When do we check in?

### 7. Red / Green Flags
What to listen for. Green: direction without prompting, unprompted updates on prior commitments, texture when describing people. Red: everything is priority, defends prior-review patterns, flat energy.

If red flags show up, name the pivot: maybe the career question is whether the current shape of the role is sustainable right now.

### 8. Questions You Owe Them
What they're likely to ask. Have an answer ready or say when you'll have one. Do not leave vague.

### 9. Flow (60 min default)
Time-boxed agenda. The template:
- 0-10: Energy and direction. Open-ended.
- 10-25: The direction question.
- 25-35: Reflect back. Align on the gap.
- 35-50: Pick the experiment. Make it specific.
- 50-60: Their questions. Write the commitment together.

### 10. Commitment Capture
Four lines to fill together at the end:
- The experiment (one sentence).
- First action by (date).
- Midpoint check (date).
- What success looks like (observable).

"If you can't fill all four lines, the conversation isn't done."

### 11. Reference
Brief summary of where they stand from the most recent review. Strengths and development areas in bullets. Self-review development themes if applicable.

## Tone for the Prep Doc

The prep is for Gabe to read 30 minutes before the meeting. It's a coach talking to a coach.

- Short sentences.
- Verbatim questions where wording matters.
- Don't hedge the read. If the person needs to hear a hard thing, the prep says so.
- No corporate softening.
- No emdashes.

---

## What Good Coaching Sounds Like (Reminders to Embed)

- "Tell me more about that."
- "What's your read on why that happened?"
- "If you had to pick one thing to change, what would it be?"
- "What would you need to see from me to make that possible?"

What it does not sound like:
- "I think you should..."
- "Here's what I'd do..."
- "Have you considered..."

Coaching pulls the decision out of them. Advising pushes it onto them. The prep reminds Gabe which mode he's in.

## After the Conversation

Update the same doc with a short "what we agreed" section at the bottom. Link the commitment into `people/TOPICS.md` if it crosses quarters or touches other people (promotion, scope change, etc.).
